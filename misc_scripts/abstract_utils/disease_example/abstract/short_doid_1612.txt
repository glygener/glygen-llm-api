The disease breast cancer (DOID:1612) is an organ system cancer that originates in the mammary gland. It is also known by several synonyms, including breast tumor, malignant neoplasm of breast, malignant tumor of the breast, mammary cancer, and mammary tumor. This type of cancer is associated with various proteins, including C-reactive protein, serum amyloid A, and carcinoembryonic antigen 5, highlighting the complexity of its pathology with a total of five significant proteins identified. Notably, breast cancer has been linked to specific glycans, with the top five being Lewis x fucosylated monogalactosylated triantennary N-glycan, monosialylated core fucosylated N-glycan, disialylated tetraantennary core fucosylated N-glycan, trisialylated Lewis fucosylated triantennary core fucosylated N-glycan, and tetrasialylated tetraantennary N-glycan. These glycans play a role in diagnostics and prognostics. Key biomarkers for breast cancer include mucin-1, mucin-16, interleukin-6, and vascular endothelial growth factor D, with each serving crucial diagnostic, prognostic, or predictive roles in the disease’s progression and treatment. Understanding these factors aids in the better management of breast cancer.
